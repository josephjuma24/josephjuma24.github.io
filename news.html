Reflections on 3 Years Researching AI for African Mobile Users: What the Data Reveals

After three years at Transsion (Carlcare) conducting AI UX research for one of Africa's most-used smartphone brands, I've accumulated a clear picture of what African mobile users actually need from on-device AI ‚Äî and how far current models fall short. The biggest gaps are not in raw model capability but in data representation, language coverage, and design assumptions. African languages are severely underrepresented in training data. Low-connectivity scenarios are treated as edge cases, not primary use cases. And culturally specific interaction patterns are largely invisible to AI systems trained on predominantly Western datasets. This piece summarizes key lessons and what I believe needs to change.

Ethical AI Is Not a Luxury ‚Äî It's Infrastructure: Lessons from African Market Research

There's a tendency in AI development to treat ethical considerations as a final-stage review ‚Äî something applied on top of a system after the core functionality is built. My experience researching AI features for African mobile users tells a different story. When AI systems lack cultural context, fail to represent local languages, or embed biases from unrepresentative training data, they don't just perform poorly ‚Äî they actively exclude. Ethical AI in this context is not a philosophical concern. It's a practical requirement for building technology that actually works for its users. This article argues for integrating ethical AI considerations as infrastructure, not afterthought.

Building AI Translation for African Languages: Challenges, Methods, and What Works

Testing AI translation models for African languages reveals a fundamental problem: most commercial systems were built for high-resource language pairs and perform significantly worse on African languages, particularly when users code-switch (mixing languages mid-sentence, a very common pattern). This article shares the research methodology I used at Transsion to evaluate translation model quality across multiple African languages ‚Äî covering accuracy, fluency, cultural appropriateness, and the specific failure modes that matter most for mobile users ‚Äî along with thoughts on what the path to genuinely inclusive multilingual AI looks like.
